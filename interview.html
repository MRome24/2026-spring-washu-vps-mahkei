In Conversation

Web Design,
Art, and
Artificial Intelligence

"To design with empathy, an understanding of the psychology that goes into design, and applying those connections into web design, is truly valuable."

Michelle Lin
Mechanical Engineer at WashU

Michelle is a mechanical engineer at Washington University in St. Louis. Beyond the classroom, she is a web designer and digital artist whose passion for design began in sixth grade. With experience in front-end development, React, and Tailwind CSS, she approaches every project at the intersection of empathy, visual storytelling, and interactive craft.

The Interview

01 Could you describe your background or experience with web design? Those first few seconds on a landing page are vital to capture interest.

Art and design have engulfed my life since I was young. I started out with digital art in 6th grade and consequently, picked up on the techniques artists use to characterize their art, which includes learning how to apply color theory and getting creative with different personal styles for artworks. In high school, I began to see students making designs for clubs, feeling inspired to share my designs with a larger group of people through club social media posts.

Those tasks are very similar to web design, something that I only began learning in my junior year of high school, but later grew into a personal hobby where I can turn a design interactive for an audience. My experience with web design currently includes front-end programming skills, React, and Tailwind CSS, small hackathon projects, and ongoing personal projects. When approaching a new project, the primary visual principles I prioritize are what the main subject of interest is and the typography that aids viewers in recognizing the message. What draws them in first when they view the landing page, and then to follow up, what is their next decision?

02 How do you navigate the relationship between aesthetic expression and user experience? Empathy is the key to understanding how to balance utility and visual art.

It's important to have a balance between these two. In web design, utility and visual art go hand in hand. User experience is always on my mind while making digital designs, and empathy is key to understanding how to achieve that. Choosing the right color palette plays a huge role in storytelling that web design depends on. Playing around with texts, objects, buttons, images, sound effects, and more, in a digital space, allows you to fully engage in the digital environments, maximizing the presence of both UX and aesthetic expression.

03 Have you utilized AI tools to assist in your web design or creative workflows? AI handles the foundation; human insight handles the empathy.

For creativity, I prefer looking at other real designs created by artists, that may be looking on Pinterest or Dribbble, to see works created by humans. AI tools, specifically Claude, have only helped me with generating fast web mockups for projects with a short timeline and assist with debugging code. Unlike other AI, Claude has the ability to build basic HTML websites that other LLMs have not been able to do yet. Building the foundations within seconds has eliminated blockage in workflows, and sometimes, with prompts, they add features that I normally wouldn't have thought of at that step.

With AI playing a role in coding, I don't believe it directly harms creativity unless it gets to a point where every step of the process depends on LLMs. For example, if I had a formatting issue in my code, I would ask Claude to detect the error and save myself time. But to design the website with empathy, an understanding of the psychology that goes into design, and taking the time to really read about the impact of different designs in the world, and applying those connections into web design, is truly valuable.

04 How do you view AI's impact on traditional artistic expression and originality in web design? Without human creativity in the loop, AI only ever generates recycled work.

As more designers start relying on AI, and solely on AI with no further contribution, it could gradually eliminate their motivations to make traditional artistic expressions. LLMs also use data from other LLMs, that data also gets used to train other models, so if we only use AI without our own creativity, it will generate recycled work. We've seen it in academia where AI hallucinates on simple prompts. Models aren't perfect right now, but as they continue to update, it can be beneficial for designers to speed up their workflow, especially for those with plans but less experienced with programming the back-end.

05 How do you see websites evolving structurally and visually over the next five to ten years? More people will publish AI-built sites, and it will be easy to tell.

Visual presentation of websites should improve in the future as students, businesses, schools, and more people are exposed to AI tools that can create complex prototypes of websites. On Facebook, I've seen a lot of schools and companies already integrating them in making announcements on posters, and while they use the same formatting and fonts that AI loves reusing, it gets the message across, and more people will inevitably use AI to create and publish websites. It may also be very noticeable to tell. Newer startups that I've seen utilize the same type of style that consists of rounded corners and minimalistic layouts; these are features that I expect to be more popular in the next few years.

06 Do you believe AI has the potential to completely replace human web designers? For now, a supplementary tool, but the risk is real if the industry stops valuing creativity.

For now, I view it as a supplementary tool. It's a saturated market for web designers right now. It makes things even riskier for designers or aspiring designers if companies and consumers do not value the importance of creativity and visual principles in society. It definitely has the potential to completely replace human designers, but some of us aren't completely satisfied with the results they are giving us as of right now, and limit it as a supplementary tool.

07 What is the most significant challenge when integrating AI-generated elements into visual design? When everything blends together, the digital environment risks becoming mundane.

The reduction in creativity is a large ethical challenge when integrating elements generated by AI into the visual design of a website. Even in bigger websites, such as Instagram or YouTube, a lot of the features are blending together with the rise in reels and shorts. All these websites have already been affected by corporations rushing to use the latest technology. With AI and the simplification of many design features, the digital environment can look more mundane in the future than it does now.
