Response 1

Reading: Restaurant Websites...

Toph Tucker makes a statement about how good restaurant websites are, and I completely agree with her. Just like Toph, before going to any restaurant, I browse their website to check out their menu and the deals they're offering. He also makes a statement about how a restaurant's website experience can be comparable to the real-life experience. I completely agree, as websites often serve as a first impression of companies, products, and individuals.

Toph also expresses a fear that restaurants will switch to CMS (content management systems)-based websites and lose the vibrancy and creativity that come from websites developed and updated manually. I agree with this to an extent, as coding your own website gives more freedom and creative control.

Reading: The New Turing Test

Ella Ullendorff starts out with a comeplling point, AI is gaslighting us. She describes a scenario all too well that feels familiar to all of us in today's time, coming across impressive works of art that end up being AI generated content. I would like to point out that this article was written in 2023, this was around when generative AI began to become mainstram. Now in 2026, AI has become integrated in our daily lives. It has it's helpful uses, but there is one thing AI takes from the individual person: creativity.

Reading 3

Response 3

Reading: Cyborgs vs. Rooms

We have seen it portrayed in multiple forms of media, the concept of human and machine as one: a cyborg. Matt Webb discusses how close we are to this concept becoming a reality. Right now, the concept of cyborgs exists at wearables, headphones, earbuds, smart glasses. But Webb contrasts this with something less talked about: room-scale computing, where the intelligence lives in the environment rather than on your body. Personally, the rooms vision makes more sense to me. Computing that fits how people already move and work together, rather than reshaping the person to fit the technology.

Reading 4

Response 4

Reading: "My website is a shifting house..."

At its core a website just a file on a server, but the more interesting question is what it means. The metaphors Laurel uses: rooms, gardens, puddles, actually reframe how I think about building for the web. A website doesn't have to be a finished product, it can be something that grows with you. What stood out to me most was the idea that having your own website is almost a form of resistance against platforms like Instagram or Facebook that are designed around ad revenue, not the user. Your own site is the one place online you actually control. As someone who builds things, that idea hits different, most of what we make lives inside someone else's system.

Reading 5

Response 5

Reading: Eno

The documentary “Eno” presents creativity as an evolving process rather than a finished product. Throughout the documentary, Brian Eno focuses on building “systems” that allow experimentation when it comes to music. He also relied on inspiration, but that was not his sole tool in creating his art. What stood out the most to me in the documentary was its “generative format.” It isn’t a fixed story, making the viewing experience unique. This approach reflects Eno’s belief that creativity comes from systems that allow variation and chance rather than absolute control.

Reading 6

Response 6

Reading: The Coming of Calm Technology

I agree with Weiser and Brown's argument that as computing becomes more embedded in everything around us, the goal shouldn't be to demand more of your attention, it should demand less. Good technology should live in the background until you actually need it. The problem with most tech today isn't that there's too much of it, it's that everything is fighting for the center of your attention at the same time, and nothing is designed to sit quietly in the periphery until it matters.

Writing Exercise 1

Excercise 1

Prompt: What is a ritual, practice, or routine in your life that is important for your psychological wellbeing and/or fulfillment? Why?

A routine in my life that is important to my psyhcological well-being is adding every event to my Google Calendar no matter how significant or miniscule it is. As a very busy person, my tasks and responsibilities can quickly overlap if my schedule is left unchecked. My callendar allows me to manage my time well and ensure I am only taking up responsibilities that I can handle. It also enables me to keep track of time and be mindful of any deadlinesoccuring. I would be a mess if I did not keep track of all my tasks within a calendar. Assignments would be late/missing, the student orgs I am could fall apart, and even social outings would fail to happen. The ritual of putting events in a calendar teaches us the aspect of maintaning balance and responsibilities. As humans, we are naturally inclined to become busy. Whether that be with jobs, friends/family, school, and extracurriculars. Our days can easily become filled with a bunch of things to do. If left untracked, we can quickly become disoriented, overwhelemed, and careless.
