Week 2  ·  Jan 20, 2026

On Contrast & Hierarchy

Reading: Lupton, Thinking with Type, Ch. 2

The reading reframed contrast for me not as a purely aesthetic choice, but as a functional one — contrast is how the eye knows where to begin. Before this, I thought of hierarchy as a grid problem. Now I see it as a tension problem: what competes, and what wins.

key idea "Contrast is not decoration — it is the syntax of visual language."

What surprised me was how much hierarchy depends on restraint. When everything is large, nothing is large. The examples in the chapter made this visceral in a way the definition alone couldn't.

Week 4  ·  Feb 3, 2026

Gestalt Principles

Reading: Ware, Visual Thinking for Design, Ch. 4

Proximity and grouping feel almost obvious once named, but I had never consciously applied them. The figure-ground examples were the most disorienting — your brain commits to one reading and resists switching even when you know both exist.

I'm curious how these principles break down in interfaces that update dynamically. If grouping is about spatial proximity, what happens when elements animate into new positions? Does the brain re-group, or does it carry the old gestalt forward?

Week 6  ·  Feb 17, 2026

Color Theory in Practice

Reading: Itten, The Art of Color, selected chapters

Itten's simultaneous contrast principle was the hardest to internalize — the idea that a color is never perceived in isolation but always relative to what surrounds it. A gray surrounded by red reads differently than the same gray surrounded by blue.

This has real implications for UI design: you can't specify a "correct" color without specifying its context. A design system that ignores this will always surprise you in production.
